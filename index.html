David Jeremiah Barry is a Guernsey-based, British value investor with a four-decade career arc spanning offshore trust administration, mineral-exploration ventures, distressed-debt litigation and concentrated equity positions in UK micro-cap companies. He operates almost exclusively through one personally-owned vehicle — Value Investments Limited (Guernsey) — and a UK holding company, DB Value Investments Limited, incorporated in 2021. His public footprint is intentionally narrow: a handful of contemporary press profiles, a 2024 podcast appearance, and statutory disclosures on Companies House and (historically) the LSE's RNS for crossings of the 3% major-holdings threshold.

The data supports a coherent picture of a single-principal, asset-discount investor operating an unleveraged, illiquid, long-duration book of UK micro- and nano-caps purchased at material discounts to tangible book value. The structure is classical Channel-Islands private investment: a Guernsey investment company, UK chartered-accountant correspondence (Brown Butler, Leeds), and a tightly held network of co-investors and advisers. There is no evidence of regulated fund management, third-party capital, retail solicitation, insolvencies, disqualifications or adverse regulatory action. Evidence-based

§ 04

Investment Strategy Signals discount-to-NAV, micro-cap, long-duration

Value Investments Limited is a Guernsey registered investment company specializing in acquiring significant holdings in UK quoted and unquoted micro-cap companies whose shares are available at prices substantially below their tangible asset value. — Self-description, valueinvestments.co.uk (archived c. 2001) CONFIRMED

The mandate is unusually pure for a private vehicle: (1) UK-listed and unlisted; (2) micro-cap; (3) significant — not passive — stake size; (4) discount to tangible book value. Each of those four criteria narrows the universe substantially. Read together with Barry's own characterisation — "I'm just investing in companies where the assets are substantially greater than the share price — preferably twice as much or more" — this is a Graham-style net-net / asset-discount strategy, applied with concentration rather than diversification.

A secondary signal is activist disposition: "Some times I try to agitate things myself a little, to make something happen." Barry's interventions are documented in three named cases — Bogod (split-capital structure), HC Slingsby (governance / disclosure), and Dart Valley Railway (tender). The pattern is constructive activism via shareholder pressure, not hostile takeover or board displacement — consistent with a single-principal holder running £-millions, not £-hundreds-of-millions.

The composite reading is of a quiet, single-principal value investor with deep professional roots in Channel Islands trust and corporate-services administration, who has spent the second half of his career running a personally-funded micro-cap book from Guernsey. The strategy is conservative in structure (no leverage, no external capital, equity-only) but contrarian in selection (deeply illiquid, asset-discounted, sometimes activist). The rare public statements — both in Citywire two decades ago and in the 2024 podcast — describe an investor who values long-term holding over portfolio churn and who reaches for unconventional value plays (defaulted 19th-century sovereign bonds; heritage-railway tender offers) where larger institutions cannot or will not look.

The structural picture is conventional and clean: Guernsey holding company, UK chartered-accountant correspondence, no charges, no insolvencies, no disqualifications, no regulatory adverse history visible. The architecture is that of a coherent personal investment platform, not a network of opaque shells. The Leeds professional-services hub (Brown Butler accountants, Fyshe Horton Finney brokers historically) provides the operational anchor that makes UK micro-cap investing possible from a Channel Islands base.

The most material gaps are: (i) no current portfolio disclosure beyond the podcast statement of "above 3% in a number" of listed companies; (ii) no visible accounts for Value Investments Limited (Guernsey company filings are not public to the same depth as UK Companies House); (iii) no documented family or succession structure, leaving open questions of long-term continuity for the vehicle.
